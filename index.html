TWO BLACK HOLES ORBIT

When two black holes orbit each other, moving closer and closer until they finally collide and merge into one larger black hole. During this powerful event, enormous energy is released; not as light, but as ripples in space itself, called gravitational waves.
STRETCHING AND SQUEEZING

Gravitational waves gently stretch and squeeze spacetime. The amount of this stretching and squeezing, called the strain h, is incredibly tiny.
GRAVITATIONAL WAVES EVERYWHERE

Gravitational waves spread and go through the entire universe, passing stars, planets, and even people; stretching and squeezing space itself
as they go.
VERY, VERY, VERY SMALL

The LIGO detectors measure a stretching & squeezing smaller than a billionth of an atom’s diameter. Over a 4-kilometer arm, the length changes by less than a thousandth of a proton’s width.
WHAT IS STRAIN h?

Strain h is not a distance. It compares how much space stretches to how large that space already is.
FROM THE EARTH TO THE SUN

If you take the distance from Earth to the Sun — about 150 million kilometers —
a passing gravitational wave would stretch and squeeze that distance by only about the width of a single human hair. That tiny change shows just how incredibly subtle gravitational waves are.
EVEN MORE PRECISE

The Einstein Telescope is a underground observatory designed to detect gravitational waves, tiny ripples in space and time caused by extreme cosmic events such as merging black holes. Thanks to its large scale, triangular design, and highly sensitive technology, it will be able to listen to the universe with greater precision than current detectors, providing new insights into the origin and
behavior of the cosmos.
